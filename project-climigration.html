Spatial Dynamics of Establishment Relocation in Relation to CBSA Climate Risk Profiles

Supervised by Professor Rachel Meltzer, Harvard University (Under Review)

This study investigates how climate risk levels and population densities within CBSAs influence the relocation patterns of business establishments over a two-decade period (2001–2021). By integrating established climate risk indices, demographic data, and advanced visualization techniques, the project aims to uncover trends and correlations that inform urban planning and economic resilience strategies.

Key Objectives

Quantify the relationship between establishment movements and CBSA climate risk indicators. Develop predictive models to forecast establishment relocations based on risk profiles. Visualize spatial patterns of business movements in relation to risk levels.

Technical Approach

Gathered datasets on business establishments, including their locations, industry classifications, and movement histories. Clustered CBSAs based on Climate Risk indicator (3 levels) and using population level (clustered to 3 levels) to control for external effect of economic factors in poplation dense areas. Tracked the movement of business establishments within and between CBSAs over the 20-year period. Focused on movements within the same population density category but across different climate risk levels. Utilized spatial lag and spatial error models to assess the influence of neighboring regions’ risk levels on establishment movements. Employed chord diagrams to illustrate the flow of business relocations between CBSAs of varying risk levels within the same population density category. Developed classification models (e.g., Random Forest, Support Vector Machines) to predict the likelihood of establishment relocation based on climate risk factors and business demographics.

Potential Application

Identified a tendency for businesses to relocate from higher-risk to lower-risk CBSAs within the same population density category. Observed that high population density areas continue to attract businesses despite varying risk levels. These insights can inform policymakers and urban planners in developing strategies to enhance economic resilience and guide infrastructure investments.
