Climate-Hydrology Analysis in Watersheds: A Multilevel Approach

Supervised by Professor Ali Ameli, University of British Columbia (Ongoing)

This project investigates hydroclimatic variability and streamflow response across different biomes using multilevel and panel regression modeling. Leveraging spatially gridded annual data on streamflow, aridity index, and seasonality indices, the analysis aims to uncover how hydroclimatic drivers interact over time and space, with a particular focus on variation across clusters (e.g., watersheds or grid codes). This work supports a better understanding of climate impacts on water systems, especially under changing environmental conditions.

Key Objectives

1. Quantify hydroclimatic trends in streamflow metrics across space (clusters/biomes) and time (years). 2. Model spatial heterogeneity by comparing random intercept and random slope models across clusters. 3. Evaluate predictors such as Aridity Index (AI), Seasonality Index (SI), and Seasonality of Flow (SF) on streamflow levels (e.g., Qmean, Q05, Q95). 4. Explore interactions and non-linear patterns between hydroclimatic variables and streamflow using panel data and mixed effects models. 5. Assess biome-level differences and how variability in predictors manifests across ecological or hydrological regions.

Technical Approach

Multilevel modeling using lme4 and lmerTest to capture nested structure (e.g., years nested within spatial units). Panel regression with fixed effects (plm) and generalized additive models (mgcv) for robustness checks. Model comparison and diagnostics using AIC, R², and residual plots (MuMIn, performance). Variance decomposition of streamflow predictors across clusters. Quantile summaries and visualizations for ecological interpretation.

Potential Application

• Hydrologic Forecasting: Improve understanding of spatially varying streamflow responses to climate, enhancing water resource modeling. • Climate Impact Assessment: Inform adaptation strategies by identifying regions most vulnerable to changes in seasonality or aridity. • Ecological and Environmental Planning: Help conservation and water management agencies account for biome-specific hydrologic patterns. • Research and Education: Provide a template for applying multilevel models to spatiotemporal environmental data.
